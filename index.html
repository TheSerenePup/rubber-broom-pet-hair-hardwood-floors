Why your regular broom is failing (and it’s not your fault)

Pet hair carries a slight negative charge. When you sweep with plastic or natural bristles, you create friction but very little static adhesion — and often you just electrify the hair so it repels from the broom. The result? Hair jumps away, floats, or clings to baseboards. Bristles scatter. Rubber attracts. It’s physics, not your cleaning skills.

How to pick the best rubber broom for your home

Look for a broom with natural or synthetic rubber that’s firm yet pliable. A squeegee edge gives you 2-in-1 versatility. Adjustable handles are a bonus for tall users. And always check that the rubber is non-marking — both options above are safe for light floors.

🚫 Avoid this common static-killer

Mistake: Using a rubber broom on wet or damp floors. Water dissipates static charge. The broom will still squeegee water, but the hair-grabbing magic vanishes. Always sweep dry. If you need to damp-mop, do it after the static sweep.

Pro tip: Use short, brisk strokes to build maximum static. Clean the rubber edge with a damp cloth after use — it’ll be loaded with hair and dust.
